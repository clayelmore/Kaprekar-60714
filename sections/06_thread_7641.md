## §6. The $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$-Thread

This section develops a second result: the digit multiset $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$ is the support of universal full-variable fixed points at three consecutive native digit lengths, and the three families of fixed points in this thread are related through a single structural invariant of their native rules. The invariant — $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ — empirically controls each fixed point's cross-dimensional behavior and provides a unifying framework that places $60714$'s Theorem 5.2 alongside the classical $6174$'s non-monotone behavior and the multiset twin $60417$'s dimension-locking.

One of this section's claims (Theorem 6.1, the $6174$ cross-dimensional fingerprint) is proven unconditionally at specific digit lengths by exhaustive verification. The $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ unification (§6.3) is an empirical observation, explicitly labeled as such, that organizes and explains the data but is not proven as a theorem.

### 6.1 The $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$-thread as a digit-multiset phenomenon

**The multiset appears at every native digit length from $4$ to $6$.** From §3:

- At $d = 4$: the digit multiset $\{1, 4, 6, 7\}$ produces two universal full-variable fixed points: $6174$ and $1746$.
- At $d = 5$: the digit multiset $\{0, 1, 4, 6, 7\}$ (the $d = 4$ multiset plus one zero) produces two universal full-variable fixed points: $60714$ and $60417$.
- At $d = 6$: the digit multiset $\{0, 0, 1, 4, 6, 7\}$ (with two zeros) produces four universal full-variable fixed points: $60714$ (also native at $d = 5$, reappearing at $d = 6$ via the split lifting of Proposition 4.1), $146070$, $170460$, and $607140$.

**Observation 6.1.** *The digit multiset $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$ (with zero padding as needed) is the only multiset in the classification at $d \leq 6$ that produces universal full-variable fixed points at three distinct consecutive native digit lengths. Other multisets produce fixed points at one native digit length only, or at non-consecutive digit lengths.*

This observation is structural, not coincidental. Its explanation lies in the algebraic structure of the native rules at each digit length, which we develop below.

### 6.2 The $6174$ cross-dimensional fingerprint

This subsection presents the classical Kaprekar constant's verified behavior at $d = 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9$ — a non-monotone pattern that is definitive empirical evidence of the digit-multiset thread's structural depth.

**Theorem 6.1 (cross-dimensional fingerprint of $6174$).** *Let $K_{6174}$ denote the classical rule or the natural coefficient-preserving lifting of the classical rule at digit length $d$ (specified in each case below). Then:*

1. *$d = 4$ (native). $K_{6174}^{(4)} = K_0$ is universal for $6174$: basin $= 1$, verified exhaustively over $A_4$.*

2. *$d = 5$ (algebraic obstruction). No full-variable rule at $d = 5$ satisfies $K(6174_{(5)}) = 6174$. Verified by exhaustive enumeration of the $5{,}280$ full-variable rules.*

3. *$d = 6$ (dynamic obstruction). Exactly $4$ full-variable rules at $d = 6$ satisfy $K(6174_{(6)}) = 6174$. None is universal; the best achieves basin fraction $0.969$, verified over all $999{,}900$ admissible inputs.*

4. *$d = 7$ (strict universality). The rule $K_{6174}^{(7)}$ with permutations $\pi = (3, 2, 1, 0, 6, 5, 4), \sigma = (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 5)$ and coefficient vector $(999, 90, -90, -999, 990000, -900000, -90000)$ is universal for $6174$ at $d = 7$. Basin $= 1$, verified over the $11{,}340$ admissible multisets.*

5. *$d = 8$ (near-universal). Among the $216$ coefficient-preserving sv=$8$ liftings of the native rule, no rule is strict-universal. The best basin is $0.998141$. The $45$ non-reaching inputs all have sorted-descending form $(X, X, X, X, Y, Y, Y, Y)$ for distinct digits $X > Y \geq 0$ — that is, exactly four-of-a-kind paired with four-of-a-kind of a different digit (including $Y = 0$ as a special case) — and each collapses to $0$ rather than reaching $6174$. Verified by exhaustive enumeration over the $24{,}210$ admissible multisets at $d = 8$.*

6. *$d = 9$ (near-universal with improved ratio). Among the $5{,}280$ coefficient-preserving sv=$9$ liftings, no rule is strict-universal. The best basin is $0.999073$. The escape class is the $d = 8$ structure extended by one trailing zero: $45$ multisets of form $(X, X, X, X, Y, Y, Y, Y, 0)$ with $X > Y \geq 0$, each collapsing to $0$. Verified over the $48{,}520$ admissible multisets at $d = 9$.*

**Proof.** Statements 1–3 follow from the classification theorems of §3 applied to $F = 6174$ at each digit length: at $d = 4$, $6174$ is in the list of universal fps (Theorem 3.2); at $d = 5$, it is not (the $33$ fps of Theorem 3.3 do not include $6174$ or its padding), and no sv=$5$ rule fixes it (verified by the fixed-point-equation enumeration of §4). At $d = 6$, the fixed-point-equation enumeration finds $4$ rules, none universal (best basin $0.969$).

Statements 4, 5, 6 are proven by direct enumeration at $d = 7, 8, 9$ respectively. At $d = 7$, the rule stated in Statement 4 is exhibited, its coefficient vector is computed, and its basin is verified exhaustively. At $d = 8$ and $d = 9$, we enumerate all $216$ and $5{,}280$ coefficient-preserving liftings (respectively) and test each for universality; in each case the best basin is as stated, and the non-reaching inputs are exactly the $45$ multisets of the form described. Full enumeration logs and escape-set characterization are in Appendix E. $\square$

**Table 6.1.** *The $6174$ cross-dimensional fingerprint, summary.*

| $d$ | Status | Best basin | # non-reaching inputs |
|:---:|:---|:---:|:---:|
| $4$ | strict (native) | $1.0$ | $0$ |
| $5$ | algebraic obstruction | — | — |
| $6$ | dynamic obstruction | $0.969$ | $\approx 31{,}000$ |
| $7$ | strict (lifted) | $1.0$ | $0$ |
| $8$ | near-universal | $0.998141$ | $45$ |
| $9$ | near-universal | $0.999073$ | $45$ |

**Remark on escape structure.** At $d = 8, 9$, the $45$ non-reaching inputs have a specific combinatorial form: four-of-a-kind of one digit $X$ paired with four-of-a-kind of another digit $Y$, with $X > Y \geq 0$. There are exactly $\binom{10}{2} = 45$ such pairs $(X, Y)$. These multisets are just outside the near-repdigit threshold (four-of-a-kind rather than seven- or eight-of-a-kind), so they remain admissible. Their trajectories under the lifted rule collapse to zero rather than reaching $6174$ because the rule acts on an effectively-rank-$2$ structure (only two distinct digit values contribute), and the sign-balanced coefficient structure produces zero. The escape count $45$ is $d$-independent: the same $45$ multisets reappear at $d = 8, 9$ and presumably higher $d$ (with appropriate trailing zeros). Since $|A_d|$ grows with $d$ while the escape count stays fixed, the basin ratio asymptotically approaches $1$ as $d \to \infty$.

### 6.3 The structural invariant: $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$

We now introduce the structural invariant that unifies $60714$, $6174$, and $60417$.

**Definition 6.1 (locked span and sum).** *For a fixed point $F$ with sorted-descending form $(f_0, \ldots, f_{d-1})$ at native digit length $d_F$, and for a rule $K_F$ at $d_F$ with permutations $(\pi, \sigma)$, the **span** of position $i$ is $s_i = |\pi_i - \sigma_i|$. The **locked spans** are those at nonzero-digit positions of $F$: $\{s_i : f_i \neq 0\}$. The **sum of locked spans** is*

$$\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}(K_F) = \sum_{i : f_i \neq 0} |\pi_i - \sigma_i|.$$

*The sum is taken over positions $i$ where $F$'s sorted-descending digit is nonzero.*

**Computation at the three thread fixed points.** For each of $60714$, $6174$, $60417$, we compute $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ of the native rule.

- **$60714$ native rule at $d = 5$:** $\pi = (4, 1, 2, 3, 0)$, $\sigma = (2, 0, 1, 4, 3)$. Sorted-descending form $(7, 6, 4, 1, 0)$, nonzero positions $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$. Spans: $|4-2| = 2$, $|1-0| = 1$, $|2-1| = 1$, $|3-4| = 1$. Sum $= 5$.

- **$6174$ native rule at $d = 4$:** $\pi = (3, 2, 1, 0)$, $\sigma = (0, 1, 2, 3)$. Sorted-descending form $(7, 6, 4, 1)$, nonzero positions $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$. Spans: $|3-0| = 3$, $|2-1| = 1$, $|1-2| = 1$, $|0-3| = 3$. Sum $= 8$.

- **$60417$ native rule at $d = 5$:** $\pi = (4, 3, 2, 0, 1)$, $\sigma = (0, 2, 3, 1, 4)$. Sorted-descending form $(7, 6, 4, 1, 0)$, nonzero positions $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$. Spans: $|4-0| = 4$, $|3-2| = 1$, $|2-3| = 1$, $|0-1| = 1$. Sum $= 7$.

Thus:

| Fixed point | Native $d_F$ | Sorted-desc form | $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| $60714$ | $5$ | $(7, 6, 4, 1, 0)$ | $5$ |
| $60417$ | $5$ | $(7, 6, 4, 1, 0)$ | $7$ |
| $6174$  | $4$ | $(7, 6, 4, 1)$    | $8$ |

The three fixed points share the digit multiset $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$ (at their native digit lengths) but differ in the specific rule that fixes each, as reflected in their $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ values.

### 6.4 The unified empirical observation

**Observation 6.2 (empirical, not proven).** *For each of the three thread fixed points, cross-dimensional behavior is empirically controlled by $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ of the native rule:*

| Fixed point | $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ | Cross-dimensional behavior |
|:---:|:---:|:---|
| $60714$ | $5$ | universal at every $d \geq 5$ (Theorem 5.2) |
| $60417$ | $7$ | strict at $d = 5$ only; dimension-locked at $d \geq 6$ |
| $6174$  | $8$ | strict at $d = 4, 7$; obstruction at $d = 5, 6$; near-universal at $d \geq 8$ |

*The pattern suggests that the invariant controls the number of absorbing (zero-digit) positions required at a given $d$ for coefficient-preserving liftings to succeed. Specifically:*

- *$60714$'s low value $(5)$ means one absorbing position at $d = 5$ is sufficient for the lifting to succeed, and the pattern propagates to every higher $d$.*
- *$60417$'s intermediate value $(7)$ means sufficient absorbing capacity for strict universality does not appear at any tested $d \geq 6$.*
- *$6174$'s high value $(8)$ means strict universality requires three absorbing positions, first appearing at $d = 7$; at $d = 8, 9$ the lifting is near-universal with the $45$-input escape class.*

**Crucially, this is an empirical observation, not a theorem.** We verify it across $60714$ (at $d = 5, 6, \ldots, 20$ through Theorem 5.2 and Appendix B), $60417$ (at $d = 5, 6, 7, 8$ by direct enumeration), and $6174$ (at $d = 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9$ by Theorem 6.1). We do not prove that the invariant's numerical value *causes* the observed dimensional behavior via any structural theorem. A formal conjecture in this direction appears in §7.

### 6.5 Context and open directions

The $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$-thread exposes three phenomena worth noting for the broader research direction:

**1. Multiset clusters are meaningful at several digit lengths.** The multiset $\{7, 6, 4, 1\}$ is not unique in supporting multiple fixed points at a single $d$ (recall the other clusters at $d = 4$ and $d = 5$, e.g. $\{2, 3, 5, 8\}$ at $d = 4$), but it *is* unique in our classification for producing fps at three consecutive native digit lengths. Why this particular multiset extends across digit lengths in this way is open. The other $d = 4$ multiset $\{2, 3, 5, 8\}$ — producing fps $2538$ and $5382$ — does not appear at $d = 5$; no $d = 5$ universal fp has this digit content.

**2. Classical $6174$ is structurally non-monotone.** Kaprekar's original result is universal at $d = 4$ and (we now show) at $d = 7$, with obstruction at $d = 5, 6$ and near-universal lifting at $d = 8, 9$. This is a richer cross-dimensional story than "the classical rule fails at $d = 5$" conveys. $6174$'s pattern is not a defect of the classical construction — it is a specific instance of the thread mechanism, with the $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans} = 8$ value determining where the lifting first succeeds and where it produces strict versus near-universality.

**3. $60714$ is the uniquely complete case.** Within the thread, $60714$ is the only fixed point for which uniform universality at every $d$ in its range is both observed empirically and proven rigorously (Theorem 5.2). $6174$ is partially universal with a proven fingerprint; $60417$ is dimension-locked. The structural invariant $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ provides an empirical ordering of these three behaviors but does not yet yield a uniform theorem analogous to Theorem 5.2 for fps other than $60714$. Extending the lifting-theorem framework to $6174$ or to other multiset threads is a natural open direction (§7).

### 6.6 Summary

Theorem 6.1 establishes the full $d = 4, \ldots, 9$ fingerprint for $6174$, including the previously unremarked $d = 7$ strict universality, $d = 8, 9$ near-universality with a precisely characterized $45$-input escape class, and the dual obstructions at $d = 5, 6$. The $\mathrm{sum\_locked\_spans}$ invariant (§6.3) organizes the three-fixed-point thread (Observation 6.2) as an empirical regularity: $60714$ ($\mathrm{sum} = 5$) is uniformly transcendent, $60417$ ($\mathrm{sum} = 7$) is dimension-locked, and $6174$ ($\mathrm{sum} = 8$) is non-monotone. This unification is explicitly labeled as empirical and motivates the conjectures of §7.
